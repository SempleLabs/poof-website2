Expense Categorization Cheat Sheet

A quick-reference guide for properly categorizing every business expense

Why Categorization Matters

Correctly categorizing expenses ensures accurate financial reports, maximizes tax deductions, and keeps you audit-ready. This cheat sheet covers the most common expense categories with examples and IRS deductibility notes.

Detailed Category Breakdowns

Advertising & Marketing

Any cost incurred to promote your business to customers.

  • Online ads (Google, Facebook, Instagram, LinkedIn)
  • Print materials (business cards, flyers, brochures)
  • Website design, hosting, and domain registration
  • SEO and content marketing services
  • Sponsorships and promotional events
  • Email marketing platforms (Mailchimp, etc.)

Car & Vehicle Expenses

You can use standard mileage (67¢/mile for 2024) or actual expenses—not both.

  • Standard mileage: Track miles driven for business purposes
  • Actual expenses: Gas, oil, repairs, tires, insurance, registration, depreciation
  • Parking fees and tolls are deductible under either method
  • Commuting from home to a regular office is not deductible
Record-keeping: Log the date, destination, business purpose, and miles for every trip. Apps and mileage trackers make this easy.

Meals & Entertainment

  • Business meals with clients or prospects — 50% deductible
  • Meals during business travel — 50% deductible
  • Office snacks and coffee for employees — 50% deductible
  • Entertainment (sporting events, concerts) — Not deductible (since 2018 tax reform)
Documentation: Record who attended, the business purpose, and the amount. Write it on the receipt or in your expense notes.

Home Office

If you use part of your home exclusively and regularly for business:

  • Simplified method: $5 per sq ft, max 300 sq ft = $1,500 max deduction
  • Regular method: Calculate the percentage of your home used for business, apply to mortgage/rent, utilities, insurance, repairs
  • Space must be your principal place of business or used to meet clients
  • A dedicated room or clearly defined workspace is ideal

Software & Subscriptions

  • Accounting/bookkeeping software (Poof, etc.)
  • Project management (Asana, Monday, Trello)
  • Design tools (Canva, Adobe Creative Cloud)
  • Communication tools (Slack, Zoom, Google Workspace)
  • Industry-specific software and cloud services
  • Website/app hosting (AWS, Vercel, etc.)

Common Categorization Mistakes

MistakeCorrect Approach
Putting everything under "Miscellaneous"Use specific categories—it maximizes deductions and makes reports useful
Categorizing owner draws as expensesOwner draws are equity transactions, not expenses
Mixing personal and business purchasesOnly categorize the business portion; split transactions if needed
Categorizing equipment as suppliesItems over $2,500 should be capitalized and depreciated (or expensed under Section 179)
Missing contractor paymentsTrack all contractor payments—you must issue 1099s for anyone paid $600+
Forgetting to split mixed-use expensesPhone, internet, car—calculate and deduct only the business percentage

IRS Red Flags to Avoid

  • Disproportionately large meals & entertainment relative to revenue
  • 100% business use of a vehicle (rare and scrutinized)
  • Large round numbers without receipts
  • Home office deduction without exclusive-use documentation
  • Excessive travel expenses for your business type

Quick Decision Flowchart

Is this expense ordinary and necessary for my business?

  • → Yes: Categorize under the most specific category available
  • → Partially: Split the expense — deduct only the business portion
  • → No: Personal expense — do not record as a business expense

Is it over $2,500?

  • → Yes: Consider capitalizing as an asset (equipment, furniture, etc.)
  • → No: Record as a current-period expense
